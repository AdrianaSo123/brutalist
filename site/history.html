FROM CONCRETE TO WEB

THE EVOLUTION FROM BRUTALISM TO NEO-BRUTALISM

A TIMELINE OF RAW AESTHETICS

Neo-Brutalism didn't emerge from nowhere. It's rooted in architectural brutalism's honest materials (1950s) and early web design's bold simplicity (1990s). This fusion creates a unique digital aesthetic that's both nostalgic and contemporary.

From Le Corbusier's raw concrete to Pascal Deville's curated web collection, trace how brutalist philosophy evolved into today's neo-brutalist digital movement.

1950s: THE BIRTH

ARCHITECTURAL ORIGINS

1947-1952

LE CORBUSIER'S UNITÉ D'HABITATION

The Swiss-French architect completes his housing complex in Marseille using raw concrete (béton brut). This becomes the foundation of Brutalist architecture—exposed materials, honest structure, no decorative facades.

1953

TERM "BRUTALISM" COINED

Alison and Peter Smithson use "Brutalism" to describe their architectural approach. The name derives from "béton brut" (raw concrete in French), emphasizing truth to materials and honest construction.

1960s-1970s: THE MOVEMENT

PEAK BRUTALISM

1960s

GLOBAL EXPANSION

Brutalist architecture spreads worldwide. Government buildings, universities, housing projects embrace the style. Raw concrete, geometric forms, exposed structure become symbols of modernist progress and social democracy.

1963-1982

BARBICAN CENTRE

London's Barbican Estate construction begins—one of the most ambitious Brutalist residential projects. Massive concrete structures, elevated walkways, uncompromising geometric design. Opens to the public in 1982.

1970s

CRITICAL BACKLASH

Brutalism faces criticism for being "ugly," "cold," "inhuman." Many buildings fall into disrepair. The style becomes associated with failed social housing and urban decay.

1980s-2000s: THE DECLINE

ARCHITECTURAL REJECTION

1980s-1990s

DEMOLITION ERA

Hundreds of Brutalist buildings demolished. Post-modernism rejects raw concrete for decorative elements. Brutalism becomes unfashionable, dismissed as architectural mistake.

1996

CRAIGSLIST LAUNCHES

Craig Newmark's classified ads site launches with pure HTML and minimal styling. While not intentionally Brutalist, it embodies the philosophy—stripped down, honest, functional. Design remains largely unchanged for decades.

2010s: DIGITAL NEO-BRUTALISM

THE FUSION EMERGES

2012-2014

REACTION TO FLAT DESIGN

Designers move beyond skeuomorphism to flat design, but some push further. Neo-brutalism emerges as designers combine brutalist honesty with 90s web nostalgia—thick borders, bright colors, blocky layouts.

2014

BRUTALIST ARCHITECTURE RENAISSANCE

Architectural appreciation grows. Books, exhibitions celebrate Brutalist buildings. Social media spreads awareness. Younger generations rediscover brutalist aesthetics.

2016

BRUTALISTWEBSITES.COM LAUNCHES

Pascal Deville creates the definitive collection of brutalist/neo-brutalist web design. Curates hundreds of sites. The term "neo-brutalism" gains recognition as distinct from pure brutalism.

2016

WASHINGTON POST ELECTION COVERAGE

The newspaper's election site uses stark black/white/red palette, minimal decoration, raw data. High-profile example of brutalist principles in mainstream media.

2020s: NEO-BRUTALISM MAINSTREAM

BRANDS EMBRACE BOLD

2020-PRESENT

BRAND ADOPTION

Major brands embrace neo-brutalism: Figma's bold rebrand, Gumroad's raw aesthetic, Balenciaga's digital presence. No longer just underground—neo-brutalism enters mainstream design.

2021-2023

DESIGN SYSTEM INTEGRATION

Neo-brutalist elements appear in component libraries: thick borders, hard shadows, bright colors. UI frameworks acknowledge the style as legitimate design language.

2025

REFINED NEO-BRUTALISM

Style matures with UX best practices. Nielsen Norman Group publishes neo-brutalism guidelines. Designers balance boldness with usability, accessibility standards with raw aesthetics.

CORE PRINCIPLES ACROSS ERAS

TRUTH TO MATERIALS

1950s: Show the concrete. Don't hide it.

2020s: Show the grid. Expose structure.

HONEST FUNCTION

1950s: Form follows function.

2020s: No unnecessary decoration.

ANTI-ESTABLISHMENT

1950s: Reject ornamental tradition.

2020s: Reject corporate polish.

DEMOCRATIC DESIGN

1950s: Architecture for the people.

2020s: Accessible, honest interfaces.
